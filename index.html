📜 MODULE 1: PHILIPPINE CONSTITUTION (1987) - COMPLETE ▼

ARTICLE I - NATIONAL TERRITORY

  • The national territory comprises the Philippine archipelago, with all the islands and waters embraced therein, and all other territories over which the Philippines has sovereignty or jurisdiction
  • Consists of terrestrial, fluvial, and aerial domains, including its territorial sea, the seabed, the subsoil, the insular shelves, and other submarine areas
  • The waters around, between, and connecting the islands of the archipelago, regardless of their breadth and dimensions, form part of the internal waters of the Philippines

ARTICLE II - DECLARATION OF PRINCIPLES AND STATE POLICIES

  • Section 1: The Philippines is a democratic and republican State. Sovereignty resides in the people and all government authority emanates from them
  • Section 2: The Philippines renounces war as an instrument of national policy, adopts the generally accepted principles of international law as part of the law of the land
  • Section 3: Civilian authority is, at all times, supreme over the military
  • Section 4: The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people
  • Section 5: The maintenance of peace and order, the protection of life, liberty, and property, and the promotion of the general welfare are essential
  • Section 6: The separation of Church and State is inviolable
  • Section 7: The State shall pursue an independent foreign policy
  • Section 8: The Philippines, consistent with the national interest, adopts and pursues a policy of freedom from nuclear weapons
  • Section 9: Just and dynamic social order that ensures prosperity and independence
  • Section 10: Social justice in all phases of national development
  • Section 11: Value of human dignity and full respect for human rights
  • Section 12: Family as foundation of the nation
  • Section 13: Role of youth in nation-building
  • Section 14: Women in nation-building
  • Section 15: Right to health
  • Section 16: Right to a balanced and healthful ecology
  • Section 17: Priority to education, science, technology, arts, culture, and sports
  • Section 18: Labor as primary social economic force
  • Section 19: Self-reliant and independent national economy
  • Section 20: Private sector as prime mover of the economy
  • Section 21: Rural development
  • Section 22: Indigenous cultural communities
  • Section 23: Non-governmental organizations
  • Section 24: Autonomy of local governments
  • Section 25: Autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and Cordilleras
  • Section 26: Equal access to public service
  • Section 27: Honesty and integrity in public service
  • Section 28: Full public disclosure of transactions involving public interest

ARTICLE III - BILL OF RIGHTS (22 Sections - COMPLETE)

  • Section 1: No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws
  • Section 2: The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects against unreasonable searches and seizures of whatever nature and for any purpose shall be inviolable, and no search warrant or warrant of arrest shall issue except upon probable cause to be determined personally by the judge after examination under oath or affirmation of the complainant and the witnesses he may produce, and particularly describing the place to be searched and the persons or things to be seized
  • Section 3: The privacy of communication and correspondence shall be inviolable except upon lawful order of the court, or when public safety or order requires otherwise as prescribed by law
  • Section 4: No law shall be passed abridging the freedom of speech, of expression, or of the press, or the right of the people peaceably to assemble and petition the government for redress of grievances
  • Section 5: No law shall be made respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof. The free exercise and enjoyment of religious profession and worship, without discrimination or preference, shall forever be allowed. No religious test shall be required for the exercise of civil or political rights
  • Section 6: The liberty of abode and of changing the same within the limits prescribed by law shall not be impaired except upon lawful order of the court. Neither shall the right to travel be impaired except in the interest of national security, public safety, or public health, as may be provided by law
  • Section 7: The right of the people to information on matters of public concern shall be recognized. Access to official records, and to documents and papers pertaining to official acts, transactions, or decisions, as well as to government research data used as basis for policy development, shall be afforded the citizen, subject to such limitations as may be provided by law
  • Section 8: The right of the people, including those employed in the public and private sectors, to form unions, associations, or societies for purposes not contrary to law shall not be abridged
  • Section 9: Private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation
  • Section 10: No law impairing the obligation of contracts shall be passed
  • Section 11: Free access to the courts and quasi-judicial bodies and adequate legal assistance shall not be denied to any person by reason of poverty
  • Section 12: Any person under investigation for the commission of an offense shall have the right to be informed of his right to remain silent and to have competent and independent counsel preferably of his own choice. If the person cannot afford the services of counsel, he must be provided with one. These rights cannot be waived except in writing and in the presence of counsel
  • Section 13: All persons, except those charged with offenses punishable by reclusion perpetua when evidence of guilt is strong, shall, before conviction, be bailable by sufficient sureties, or be released on recognizance as may be provided by law. The right to bail shall not be impaired even when the privilege of the writ of habeas corpus is suspended
  • Section 14: No person shall be held to answer for a criminal offense without due process of law. In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall be presumed innocent until the contrary is proved, and shall enjoy the right to be heard by himself and counsel, to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation against him, to have a speedy, impartial, and public trial, to meet the witnesses face to face, and to have compulsory process to secure the attendance of witnesses and the production of evidence in his behalf
  • Section 15: The privilege of the writ of habeas corpus shall not be suspended except in cases of invasion or rebellion when the public safety requires it
  • Section 16: All persons shall have the right to a speedy disposition of their cases before all judicial, quasi-judicial, or administrative bodies
  • Section 17: No person shall be compelled to be a witness against himself
  • Section 18: No person shall be detained solely by reason of his political beliefs and aspirations. No involuntary servitude in any form shall exist except as a punishment for a crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted
  • Section 19: Excessive fines shall not be imposed, nor cruel, degrading or inhuman punishment inflicted. Neither shall death penalty be imposed, unless, for compelling reasons involving heinous crimes, the Congress hereafter provides for it
  • Section 20: No person shall be imprisoned for debt or non-payment of a poll tax
  • Section 21: No person shall be twice put in jeopardy of punishment for the same offense. If an act is punished by a law and an ordinance, conviction or acquittal under either shall constitute a bar to another prosecution for the same act
  • Section 22: No ex post facto law or bill of attainder shall be enacted

ARTICLE VI - LEGISLATIVE DEPARTMENT

  • The legislative power shall be vested in the Congress of the Philippines which shall consist of a Senate and a House of Representatives
  • Senate: Composed of 24 Senators elected at large, term of 6 years, not more than 2 consecutive terms
  • House of Representatives: Not more than 250 members, term of 3 years, not more than 3 consecutive terms
  • Party-list system to represent marginalized sectors
  • Powers: Enact laws, appropriate funds, tax, concur with treaties, declare war

ARTICLE VII - EXECUTIVE DEPARTMENT

  • The executive power shall be vested in the President of the Philippines
  • President and Vice-President: Elected by direct vote, term of 6 years
  • President not eligible for reelection, Vice-President maximum of 2 consecutive terms
  • Qualifications: Natural-born citizen, registered voter, able to read and write, 40 years old on day of election, resident for at least 10 years
  • Powers: Control of executive departments, military command, pardoning power, diplomatic power, budgetary power

ARTICLE VIII - JUDICIAL DEPARTMENT

  • Judicial power vested in Supreme Court and lower courts established by law
  • Supreme Court composed of a Chief Justice and 14 Associate Justices
  • Qualifications: Natural-born citizen, 40 years old, 15 years judge or lawyer
  • Powers: Judicial review, original and appellate jurisdiction, administrative supervision over courts

ARTICLE IX - CONSTITUTIONAL COMMISSIONS

  • A. Civil Service Commission (CSC): Administer civil service, appointments, examinations
  • B. Commission on Elections (COMELEC): Enforce election laws, registration, canvassing
  • C. Commission on Audit (COA): Examine government funds and accounts
  • Each commission composed of a Chairman and 2 Commissioners, term of 7 years

ARTICLE X - LOCAL GOVERNMENT

  • Territorial and political subdivisions: provinces, cities, municipalities, barangays
  • Autonomy to local government units
  • Creation, division, merger, abolition subject to criteria established by law

ARTICLE XI - ACCOUNTABILITY OF PUBLIC OFFICERS

  • Public office is a public trust
  • Ombudsman (Tanodbayan): Investigate public officials, prosecute cases
  • Impeachment: Grounds - culpable violation of Constitution, treason, bribery, graft and corruption, other high crimes, betrayal of public trust
  • Impeachable officers: President, Vice-President, Supreme Court Justices, Constitutional Commission members, Ombudsman
  • House initiates impeachment, Senate tries impeachment cases

ARTICLE XII - NATIONAL ECONOMY AND PATRIMONY

  • Filipino ownership of natural resources
  • Protection of Filipino enterprises
  • Regulation of foreign investments

ARTICLE XIII - SOCIAL JUSTICE AND HUMAN RIGHTS

  • Protection of labor, agrarian reform, urban land reform, housing, health, women, indigenous peoples
  • Commission on Human Rights (CHR): Investigate human rights violations, provide legal aid, monitor compliance

ARTICLE XIV - EDUCATION, SCIENCE, TECHNOLOGY, ARTS, CULTURE, SPORTS

  • Right to quality education
  • Filipino as national language
  • Promotion of science and technology

ARTICLE XV - THE FAMILY

  • Recognition of the family as basic social institution
  • Protection of marriage, children, working mothers

ARTICLE XVI - GENERAL PROVISIONS

  • Flag, anthem, Armed Forces, police, mass media, advertising

ARTICLE XVII - AMENDMENTS OR REVISIONS

  • By Congress (3/4 vote), Constitutional Convention, or People's Initiative (petition of 12% of registered voters)

ARTICLE XVIII - TRANSITORY PROVISIONS

  • Transition from revolutionary government
  • Existing laws remain effective until amended
⚖️ MODULE 2: R.A. 6713 - CODE OF CONDUCT AND ETHICAL STANDARDS (COMPLETE) ▼

THE 8 NORMS OF CONDUCT (DETAILED)

  • 1. Commitment to Public Interest: Always uphold public interest over personal interest. Public officials must prioritize the welfare of the people above their own. Any action that benefits private interests at the expense of public welfare is prohibited.
  • 2. Professionalism: Perform duties with competence, integrity, and excellence. Continuously improve knowledge and skills. Deliver work of the highest quality. Meet deadlines and standards.
  • 3. Justness and Sincerity: Act with fairness and sincerity in all transactions. Treat everyone equally without discrimination. Be honest and transparent in all dealings.
  • 4. Political Neutrality: Do not engage in partisan political activities while on duty. Do not use government resources for political campaigns. Serve all citizens regardless of political affiliation.
  • 5. Responsiveness to the Public: Provide prompt, courteous, and efficient service. Respond to public inquiries and requests. Make government services accessible to all.
  • 6. Nationalism and Patriotism: Prioritize national interests over personal or sectoral interests. Promote Filipino culture and values. Support national development goals.
  • 7. Commitment to Democracy: Uphold democratic principles and processes. Respect the rule of law. Support democratic institutions.
  • 8. Simple Living: Avoid extravagant lifestyle. Live modestly in accordance with income. Do not display wealth ostentatiously. Set a good example for the community.

SALN (STATEMENT OF ASSETS, LIABILITIES, AND NET WORTH) - COMPLETE RULES

  • Filing Deadlines:
    • Within 30 days after assumption of office
    • On or before April 30 of every year thereafter
    • Within 30 days after separation from service
  • Contents:
    • Real property (land, buildings) - description, location, acquisition cost
    • Personal property (vehicles, jewelry, appliances, investments)
    • Liabilities (loans, mortgages, payables)
    • Business interests and financial connections
    • Net worth (Assets - Liabilities)
  • Public Access: SALN is available for public scrutiny. Any citizen can request to examine SALN of public officials.
  • Penalties: Failure to file SALN is grounds for administrative disciplinary action.

PROHIBITED ACTS AND PRACTICES

  • Receiving Gifts: Accepting gifts, gratuities, or favors from persons with government transactions
  • Private Practice: Engaging in private practice of profession without authority
  • Confidential Information: Disclosure or misuse of confidential information
  • Nepotism: Appointing relatives within third degree of consanguinity
  • Discrimination: Denying service based on gender, religion, ethnicity, or political affiliation
  • Conflict of Interest: Participating in transactions where they have personal interest

DUTIES OF PUBLIC OFFICIALS

  • Act immediately on public concerns
  • Process documents without delay
  • Hear parties before making decision
  • Issue certificates within 15 days
  • Submit performance reports regularly
  • Post visible service standards
Remember: RA 6713 applies to all public officials and employees, including those in government-owned and controlled corporations (GOCCs) and their subsidiaries.

UNIVERSAL DECLARATION OF HUMAN RIGHTS (UDHR) - 1948

  • Adopted: December 10, 1948 by the UN General Assembly in Paris
  • Purpose: First global statement of fundamental human rights to be universally protected
  • Structure: 30 articles covering civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights

KEY ARTICLES OF UDHR

  • Article 1: All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights
  • Article 2: Rights without distinction of any kind (race, color, sex, language, religion, etc.)
  • Article 3: Right to life, liberty, and security of person
  • Article 4: Freedom from slavery and servitude
  • Article 5: Freedom from torture and cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment
  • Article 6: Right to recognition everywhere as a person before the law
  • Article 7: All are equal before the law and entitled to equal protection
  • Article 8: Right to effective remedy by competent tribunals
  • Article 9: Freedom from arbitrary arrest, detention, or exile
  • Article 10: Right to fair public hearing by independent tribunal
  • Article 11: Presumption of innocence until proved guilty
  • Article 12: Right to privacy, family, home, correspondence, and reputation
  • Article 13: Right to freedom of movement and residence
  • Article 14: Right to seek asylum from persecution
  • Article 15: Right to nationality
  • Article 16: Right to marry and found a family
  • Article 17: Right to own property
  • Article 18: Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
  • Article 19: Freedom of opinion and expression
  • Article 20: Right to peaceful assembly and association
  • Article 21: Right to participate in government and equal access to public service
  • Article 22: Right to social security and economic, social, cultural rights
  • Article 23: Right to work, just conditions, equal pay, and form unions
  • Article 24: Right to rest and leisure
  • Article 25: Right to adequate standard of living (food, clothing, housing, medical care)
  • Article 26: Right to education
  • Article 27: Right to participate in cultural life
  • Article 28: Right to social and international order for these rights
  • Article 29: Duties to community and limitations of rights
  • Article 30: Rights cannot be used to destroy these rights

INTERNATIONAL COVENANTS (1966)

  • ICCPR (International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights):
    • Right to life, freedom from torture, freedom from slavery
    • Right to liberty and security, fair trial, recognition before law
    • Freedom of movement, thought, conscience, religion
    • Freedom of expression, assembly, association
    • Right to participate in public affairs, minority rights
    • First Optional Protocol: Individual complaints mechanism
  • ICESCR (International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights):
    • Right to work, just conditions, form unions
    • Right to social security, family protection
    • Right to adequate standard of living (food, clothing, housing)
    • Right to health, education, participation in cultural life
  • Both entered into force in 1976

PHILIPPINE COMMISSION ON HUMAN RIGHTS (CHR)

  • Created: Under Article XIII, Section 17 of the 1987 Constitution
  • Composition: Chairman and 4 Commissioners
  • Independence: Independent office, not under any branch of government
  • Powers and Functions:
    • Investigate all forms of human rights violations involving civil and political rights
    • Adopt operational guidelines and rules of procedure
    • Provide legal aid services to victims of human rights violations
    • Monitor government compliance with international human rights treaties
    • Visit prisons and detention centers
    • Recommend measures to promote human rights
    • Conduct research and education on human rights
  • Limitations: CHR cannot prosecute cases - it recommends to the prosecutor. It has no power to issue arrest warrants.

PEACE CONCEPTS AND EDUCATION

  • Negative Peace: Absence of war, armed conflict, or direct violence. The basic concept of peace as "no war."
  • Positive Peace: Presence of justice, equality, social harmony, and conditions that eliminate root causes of conflict. Includes economic justice, political participation, cultural respect.
  • Structural Violence: Systematic inequalities and injustices embedded in social, political, and economic structures that harm people (poverty, discrimination, lack of access to education/healthcare).
  • Cultural Violence: Aspects of culture (religion, ideology, language, art) that justify or legitimize direct or structural violence.
  • Peace Education: Teaching and learning processes that promote:
    • Conflict resolution and transformation
    • Tolerance and respect for diversity
    • Human rights awareness
    • Disarmament and non-violence
    • Environmental sustainability
    • Global citizenship

OTHER HUMAN RIGHTS INSTRUMENTS

  • CEDAW: Convention on Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women
  • CRC: Convention on the Rights of the Child
  • CAT: Convention Against Torture
  • CRPD: Convention on Rights of Persons with Disabilities
  • ICERD: International Convention on Elimination of Racial Discrimination
  • Geneva Conventions: International humanitarian law during armed conflict
🌳 MODULE 4: ENVIRONMENTAL LAWS AND PROTECTION (COMPLETE) ▼

MAJOR ENVIRONMENTAL LAWS - COMPLETE DETAILS

PD 1586: Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) System (1978)

  • Requires Environmental Impact Assessment for projects that significantly affect the environment
  • Projects classified as Environmentally Critical Projects (ECP) or located in Environmentally Critical Areas (ECA) must secure ECC (Environmental Compliance Certificate)
  • ECC is issued by DENR-EMB after review of EIS
  • Purpose: To prevent, minimize, or mitigate adverse environmental impacts

RA 8749: Philippine Clean Air Act of 1999

  • Comprehensive air quality management program
  • Sets National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS) for criteria pollutants
  • Requires emission standards for vehicles and industries
  • Mandates regular vehicle emission testing
  • Prohibits smoking in public places
  • Establishes Air Quality Management Fund
  • Promotes use of clean fuels and technologies

RA 9003: Ecological Solid Waste Management Act of 2000

  • Waste Segregation: Mandatory segregation at source (biodegradable, recyclable, residual, special)
  • Collection and Transport: Separate collection for different waste types
  • Recycling Program: Establishment of Materials Recovery Facilities (MRF) in every barangay
  • Composting: Promotion of composting of biodegradable waste
  • Sanitary Landfills: Closure of open dumpsites, conversion to controlled disposal facilities, establishment of sanitary landfills
  • LGU Responsibility: LGUs primarily responsible for solid waste management in their areas
  • Penalties: Fines and imprisonment for violations (open burning, littering, improper disposal)
  • Zero Waste: Promotion of zero waste lifestyle and circular economy

RA 9275: Philippine Clean Water Act of 2004

  • Water quality management framework
  • Designation of Water Quality Management Areas (WQMA)
  • Requires wastewater treatment for industries and establishments
  • Prohibits dumping of pollutants into water bodies
  • Establishes Water Quality Management Fund
  • Requires Water Quality Permits and Discharge Permits
  • Empowers LGUs to enforce water quality standards

RA 9729: Climate Change Act of 2009

  • Mainstream climate change adaptation into government policies
  • Establishes Climate Change Commission (CCC) as lead policy-making body
  • Requires Local Climate Change Action Plans (LCCAP)
  • National Framework Strategy on Climate Change
  • Prioritizes adaptation and mitigation measures

RA 10121: Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act of 2010

  • Comprehensive DRRM framework
  • Establishes National DRRM Council (NDRRMC)
  • Requires Local DRRM Plans and Offices (LDRRMO)
  • Four Thematic Areas: Prevention and Mitigation, Preparedness, Response, Rehabilitation and Recovery
  • Establishes DRRM Fund (Local and National)
  • Community-based and inclusive approach

OTHER ENVIRONMENTAL LAWS

  • RA 9147: Wildlife Resources Conservation and Protection Act
  • RA 9175: Chain Saw Act - regulates ownership and use of chainsaws
  • RA 7942: Philippine Mining Act of 1995 - regulates mining operations
  • RA 7076: People's Small-Scale Mining Act
  • RA 7586: National Integrated Protected Areas System (NIPAS) Act
  • RA 8550: Philippine Fisheries Code
  • RA 8435: Agriculture and Fisheries Modernization Act

ENVIRONMENTAL PRINCIPLES AND CONCEPTS

  • Polluter Pays Principle: Those who cause pollution or environmental damage should bear the cost of remediation and compensation. Internalize environmental costs.
  • Precautionary Principle: Lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as reason to postpone cost-effective measures to prevent environmental degradation. Better safe than sorry.
  • Sustainable Development: Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Three pillars: economic, social, environmental.
  • Intergenerational Responsibility: Duty to protect and preserve the environment for future generations. We hold the environment in trust for those who come after us.
  • Public Trust Doctrine: Government holds natural resources in trust for the people. Cannot alienate or dispose of them irresponsibly.
  • Prevention Principle: Prevention of environmental harm is better than cure. Anticipate and prevent environmental damage.
  • Environmental Impact Assessment: Systematic process to evaluate potential environmental impacts before project implementation.
  • Pollution Prevention: Reducing or eliminating pollution at source rather than treating after generation.
  • Waste Hierarchy: Prevent, Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Recover, Dispose (in order of priority).
  • Extended Producer Responsibility: Producers responsible for entire lifecycle of products including post-consumer stage.

KEY AGENCIES

  • DENR: Department of Environment and Natural Resources - primary environment agency
  • EMB: Environmental Management Bureau - under DENR, implements environmental laws
  • CCC: Climate Change Commission - climate policy
  • NDRRMC: National Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council
  • LLDA: Laguna Lake Development Authority - manages Laguna Lake region

ENGLISH GRAMMAR RULES - COMPREHENSIVE

SUBJECT-VERB AGREEMENT

  • Singular subject takes singular verb: The student studies. She writes.
  • Plural subject takes plural verb: The students study. They write.
  • Compound subjects joined by AND: Take plural verb (John and Mary are coming).
  • Compound subjects joined by OR/NOR: Verb agrees with nearer subject (Neither the teacher nor the students were present).
  • Intervening phrases don't affect agreement: The box of chocolates is (not are) on the table.
  • Indefinite pronouns (everyone, somebody, each): Take singular verb (Everyone is invited).
  • Collective nouns (team, family, committee): Singular if acting as unit, plural if individuals (The team is winning. The team are arguing among themselves).
  • Titles and names: Take singular verb (The Philippines is a country).
  • Amounts and measurements: Usually singular (Five pesos is enough).

PRONOUN-ANTECEDENT AGREEMENT

  • Pronoun must agree with antecedent in number, gender, and person
  • Singular: Each student must bring his or her own book.
  • Plural: All students must bring their books.
  • Indefinite pronouns: Everyone should do his/her best (or their in modern usage).

VERB TENSES

  • Present: Action happening now or regularly (I study every day)
  • Past: Action already happened (I studied yesterday)
  • Future: Action will happen (I will study tomorrow)
  • Present Perfect: Action started in past, continues or recently finished (I have studied for three hours)
  • Past Perfect: Action completed before another past action (I had studied before the exam started)
  • Future Perfect: Action will be completed by a future time (I will have studied by tomorrow)
  • Present Continuous: Action happening now (I am studying)
  • Past Continuous: Action was happening at a specific past time (I was studying when you called)
  • Future Continuous: Action will be happening at a specific future time (I will be studying at 8 PM)

ACTIVE AND PASSIVE VOICE

  • Active Voice: Subject performs action (The dog chased the cat)
  • Passive Voice: Subject receives action (The cat was chased by the dog)
  • Use active voice for: Direct, strong, clear sentences
  • Use passive voice for: Emphasizing receiver, unknown doer, scientific writing (The experiment was conducted)

MODIFIERS AND DANGLING MODIFIERS

  • Place modifiers close to words they modify
  • Correct: I saw a dog on the way to school.
  • Dangling modifier: Walking to school, the dog was seen. (Who was walking?)
  • Corrected: Walking to school, I saw a dog.

PARALLEL STRUCTURE

  • Use same grammatical form for equal ideas
  • Not parallel: She likes reading, to swim, and jogging.
  • Parallel: She likes reading, swimming, and jogging.
  • Parallel: She likes to read, to swim, and to jog.

COMMONLY CONFUSED WORDS

  • Effect/Affect: Effect (noun - result), Affect (verb - to influence)
  • Their/There/They're: Their (possessive), There (place), They're (they are)
  • Your/You're: Your (possessive), You're (you are)
  • Its/It's: Its (possessive), It's (it is)
  • To/Too/Two: To (preposition), Too (also/excessively), Two (number)
  • Then/Than: Then (time), Than (comparison)
  • Loose/Lose: Loose (not tight), Lose (mislay, fail to win)
  • Accept/Except: Accept (receive), Except (excluding)
  • Advice/Advise: Advice (noun), Advise (verb)
  • Complement/Compliment: Complement (complete), Compliment (praise)
  • Principal/Principle: Principal (head of school, main), Principle (rule, belief)
  • Stationary/Stationery: Stationary (not moving), Stationery (writing materials)

FILIPINO GRAMMAR - MGA BATAYANG KAALAMAN

  • Pang-uri (Adjectives): Naglalarawan sa pangngalan at panghalip
    • Mga halimbawa: maganda, malaki, mabait, matalino, masipag
    • Mga kaantasan: lantay (maganda), pahambing (mas maganda), pasukdol (pinakamaganda)
  • Pang-abay (Adverbs): Naglalarawan sa pandiwa, pang-uri, at kapwa pang-abay
    • Mga halimbawa: mabilis, dahan-dahan, palagi, bukas, dito
    • Mga uri: pamaraan (paano), panlunan (saan), pamanahon (kailan)
  • Pandiwa (Verbs): Salitang kilos o galaw
    • Mga halimbawa: tumakbo, kumain, natulog, maglalaro
    • Mga aspekto: naganap (ontingnan), nagaganap (inatingnan), magaganap (titingnan)
  • Panghalip (Pronouns): Pamalit sa pangngalan
    • Panao: ako, ikaw, siya, tayo, kayo, sila
    • Paari: akin, iyo, kanya, atin, inyo, kanila
    • Pananong: sino, ano, alin, kanino
    • Pamatlig: ito, iyan, iyon, dito, diyan, doon
  • Pang-ukol (Prepositions): sa, para sa, ayon sa, laban sa, ukol sa
  • Pangatnig (Conjunctions): at, pati, saka, o, ni, ngunit, subalit, datapwat, kung, kapag, upang
  • Pang-angkop (Linkers): na, -ng, -g

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION

  • Chronological Order: Events in time sequence (first, next, then, finally)
  • Spatial Order: Physical arrangement (left to right, top to bottom, inside to outside)
  • Order of Importance: Most to least important or vice versa
  • Cause and Effect: Reasons and results (because, therefore, as a result)
  • Problem-Solution: Identify problem then propose solution
  • Compare and Contrast: Similarities and differences

READING COMPREHENSION STRATEGIES

  • Main Idea: What the passage is mostly about
  • Supporting Details: Facts that explain or prove main idea
  • Inference: Reading between the lines, conclusion not directly stated
  • Author's Purpose: Inform, persuade, entertain, describe
  • Tone and Mood: Author's attitude, emotional atmosphere
  • Fact vs Opinion: Can be verified vs personal belief
  • Context Clues: Use surrounding words to understand unfamiliar terms

BASIC OPERATIONS & ORDER OF OPERATIONS (PEMDAS)

+ Addition − Subtraction × Multiplication ÷ Division

FRACTIONS — COMPLETE GUIDE

TYPES OF FRACTIONS

  • Proper Fraction: Numerator < Denominator — 34, 25, 78
  • Improper Fraction: Numerator ≥ Denominator — 74, 95, 113
  • Mixed Number: Whole number + proper fraction — 134, 212, 523
  • Unit Fraction: Numerator = 1 — 12, 13, 14
  • Equivalent Fractions: Same value, different form — 12 = 24 = 36 = 48

SIMPLIFYING (REDUCING) FRACTIONS

Divide numerator and denominator by their GCF (Greatest Common Factor).
  • Example: Reduce 1218 — GCF of 12 and 18 is 6 → 12 ÷ 618 ÷ 6 = 23
  • Example: Reduce 3045 — GCF = 15 → 23

CONVERTING: MIXED NUMBER ↔ IMPROPER FRACTION

Mixed → Improper:
Multiply whole × denominator, add numerator
235 = (2 × 5) + 35 = 135
Improper → Mixed:
Divide numerator by denominator
174 = 17 ÷ 4 = 4 remainder 1 = 414

ADDITION & SUBTRACTION OF FRACTIONS

Rule: Fractions must have the same denominator (LCD) before adding or subtracting.
  • Same denominator: 37 + 27 = 3 + 27 = 57
  • Different denominators — find LCD: 14 + 16 → LCD = 12 → 312 + 212 = 512
  • Subtraction: 34 − 16 → LCD = 12 → 912 − 212 = 712
  • Mixed numbers: 213 + 114 → LCD = 12 → 2412 + 1312 = 3712

MULTIPLICATION OF FRACTIONS

Rule: Multiply numerator × numerator, denominator × denominator. Simplify if possible.
  • 23 × 35 = 2 × 33 × 5 = 615 = 25
  • Whole number × fraction: 4 × 38 = 4 × 38 = 128 = 32 = 112
  • Mixed numbers — convert first: 112 × 223 = 32 × 83 = 246 = 4

DIVISION OF FRACTIONS

Rule: Multiply by the reciprocal (flip the divisor). KCF: Keep, Change, Flip.
  • 34 ÷ 25 = 34 × 52 = 158 = 178
  • 6 ÷ 34 = 61 × 43 = 243 = 8

COMPARING FRACTIONS

  • Same denominator: Compare numerators directly — 58 > 38
  • Different denominators: Convert to LCD, then compare — 34 vs 56 → LCD=12 → 912 vs 1012 → 56 is larger
  • Cross-multiply method: 23 vs 35 → 2×5=10 vs 3×3=9 → 10>9 → 23 is larger

FRACTION OF A NUMBER (FINDING PARTS)

  • Formula: Part = ab × Whole
  • Example: 34 of 80 = 34 × 80 = 60
  • Example: 25 of 150 = 25 × 150 = 60

LOGICAL REASONING - COMPLETE GUIDE

DEDUCTIVE REASONING

  • Moves from general to specific
  • Syllogism:
    • Major premise: All men are mortal
    • Minor premise: Socrates is a man
    • Conclusion: Therefore, Socrates is mortal
  • If premises are true, conclusion must be true

INDUCTIVE REASONING

  • Moves from specific to general
  • Example: The sun rose today, yesterday, always → sun always rises
  • Conclusion is probable, not certain

CONDITIONAL STATEMENTS (If-Then)

  • If p, then q (p → q)
  • Converse: If q, then p (not necessarily true)
  • Inverse: If not p, then not q (not necessarily true)
  • Contrapositive: If not q, then not p (always true)
  • Example: If it rains (p), ground gets wet (q)
    • Converse: If ground wet, it rained? (False - could be hose)
    • Contrapositive: If ground not wet, it did not rain (True)

LOGICAL FALLACIES

  • Ad Hominem: Attacking person instead of argument
  • Straw Man: Misrepresenting argument to attack it
  • False Cause: Assuming correlation means causation
  • Hasty Generalization: Conclusion from insufficient evidence
  • Circular Reasoning: Premise assumes conclusion
  • Appeal to Authority: Using authority in irrelevant field
  • False Dilemma: Presenting only two options when more exist
  • Slippery Slope: One step leads inevitably to extreme outcome

ASSUMPTIONS AND CONCLUSIONS

IDENTIFYING ASSUMPTIONS

  • Assumption: Unstated premise that must be true for argument to hold
  • Example: "She graduated with honors, so she is intelligent."
    • Assumption: Honors graduates are intelligent
    • Without this, argument fails
  • Example: "He's a lawyer, so he must be rich."
    • Assumption: All lawyers are rich

DRAWING CONCLUSIONS

  • Conclusion: Main point or inference from premises
  • Example: All mammals have lungs. Whales are mammals. Therefore, whales have lungs.
  • Example: If it rains, event cancels. It rained. Therefore, event cancelled.

STRENGTHENING AND WEAKENING ARGUMENTS

  • Strengthen: Provide evidence supporting conclusion
  • Weaken: Provide evidence contradicting assumption or link
  • Example: "Studying improves grades."
    • Strengthen: Students who study more have higher grades
    • Weaken: Some students who study get low grades

NECESSARY VS SUFFICIENT CONDITIONS

  • Necessary: Must be present for outcome (but alone not enough)
  • Sufficient: Guarantees outcome (may not be necessary)
  • Example: To be a lawyer:
    • Necessary: Pass bar exam
    • Sufficient: Graduate law, pass bar, take oath, etc.

FILING RULES - COMPREHENSIVE

BASIC ALPHABETICAL FILING

  • Compare word by word, then letter by letter
  • "Nothing comes before something" - shorter name comes first if same beginning
  • Example: Smith comes before Smithson

NAMES WITH TITLES

  • Ignore titles (Mr., Mrs., Dr., Prof.) when filing
  • File by last name, then first name
  • Example: Dr. Jose Rizal → File as: Rizal, Jose
  • Example: Mr. Juan Dela Cruz → File as: Dela Cruz, Juan

NAMES WITH PREFIXES (Mc, Mac, O', D', etc.)

  • File as spelled (McDonald, MacDonald are different)
  • Mc and Mac are considered as part of the name, not separate
  • Order: Mac comes before Mc? Actually file letter by letter:
    • MacDonald
    • Macario
    • McDonald
    • McKinley

NAMES WITH "ST." OR "SAINT"

  • File "St." as if spelled "Saint"
  • Example: St. Paul → File as: Saint Paul
  • Example: St. Peter → File as: Saint Peter
  • Order: Saint Peter comes before Saint Paul (Peter vs Paul)

NAMES WITH NUMBERS

  • File numbers as if spelled out
  • Example: 10th Avenue → File as: Tenth Avenue
  • Example: 1st Street → File as: First Street

GOVERNMENT AGENCIES AND ORGANIZATIONS

  • File under the most distinctive word
  • Example: Department of Education → File under: Education
  • Example: Commission on Human Rights → File under: Human Rights
  • Example: University of the Philippines → File under: Philippines, University

COMPANY NAMES

  • File as written, ignoring "The", "A", "An" at beginning
  • Example: The Manila Times → File as: Manila Times
  • Example: A1 Services → File as: A1 Services

HYPHENATED NAMES

  • Consider hyphenated names as one unit
  • Example: Maria Santos-Diaz → File as: Santos-Diaz, Maria
  • Example: Jose Reyes-Cruz → File as: Reyes-Cruz, Jose
